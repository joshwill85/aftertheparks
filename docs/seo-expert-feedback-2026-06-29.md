# 1. Executive SEO diagnosis

## What After the Parks already does well

The homepage has a strong, clear H1: **“Disney resort activities, today and tonight.”** It immediately explains the value proposition: users can find current Disney resort movies, campfires, crafts, pool games, fitness, and free activities outside the parks, with source dates and weather-aware filters. It also shows a freshness panel with a recent verification date, verified activity rows, and official-source count. ([After the Parks][2])

The site’s core product is excellent for search because it is **query-matching utility content**. The activities index lets users browse current activities by resort, time, type, cost, weather fit, no-park-day intent, free-only filters, reservation needs, indoor options, transportation mode, and category. ([After the Parks][3]) The resorts index also covers all 31 Disney-owned and operated Walt Disney World resort hotels and frames the page around current activities, tonight’s options, free activities, weather notes, and source freshness. ([After the Parks][4])

That is a meaningful SEO advantage. Most competitors publish static articles, copied recreation-calendar images, or broad “things to do” lists. After the Parks can answer the user’s actual question: **“What can I do today or tonight, at or near my resort, without a park ticket?”**

## The main SEO problem

The site looks very promising, but it also has symptoms of a young or partially programmatic content system: repeated templated text, occasional malformed snippets, inconsistent counts, and faceted URLs that may create indexation clutter.

Examples I found:

The All-Star Music page says no verified tonight listing is currently tracked, but the same page later shows current evening activities, including Movie Under the Stars, a glow party, and campfire timing. That kind of contradiction can hurt user trust and make Google less confident in the page’s quality. ([After the Parks][5])

Some pages contain malformed or obviously generated language, such as “Family Activities And Crafts is a activity activity,” “Other activity activities,” truncated phone/source text, odd OCR-like phrasing, and internal planning-language such as “Fixed-time activities should not receive exact timing recommendations.” ([After the Parks][6])

I also found Google-indexed or discoverable parameter-style activity URLs, including filtered activity pages such as free activities, resort-specific free filters, and category filters. Some of these should rank, but many combinations should not be indexed because they can create duplicate or near-duplicate pages. Google’s own guidance warns that duplicate pages across multiple URLs can waste crawl resources, and it recommends canonical URLs for duplicated content. ([After the Parks][7])

# 5. Technical SEO audit

## 5.1 Crawl and indexation

### What looks good

The site is publicly indexed and has crawlable pages for the homepage, activities, resorts, guides, specific resort pages, and activity pages. The core page types are the right ones:

* Homepage: current resort activities today/tonight. ([After the Parks][2])
* Resorts index: all 31 Disney World resort activity calendars. ([After the Parks][4])
* Activities index: filterable current activity database. ([After the Parks][3])
* Guides index: topical planning guides for no-park days, rainy days, free activities, first nights, resort hopping, and audience-specific planning. ([After the Parks][8])

### What needs fixing

The biggest technical SEO risk is **uncontrolled faceted indexation**. Search results show indexed parameter pages such as filtered activity URLs for free activities, resort-specific free activities, and category filters. ([After the Parks][7])

You should divide filtered pages into three buckets:

### Indexable

Create clean, static, optimized URLs for high-value searches:

* `/disney-world-resort-activity-calendars/`
* `/disney-resort-activities-today/`
* `/disney-resort-activities-tonight/`
* `/disney-world-resort-movie-schedule/`
* `/disney-resort-campfire-schedule/`
* `/free-disney-resort-activities/`
* `/rainy-day-disney-resort-activities/`
* `/disney-world-no-park-day/`
* `/disney-resort-hopping-itinerary/`

### Canonicalized

Useful filtered views that duplicate the main activity database should canonical back to a parent page or to a clean equivalent. For example:

* `/activities?free=true` → canonical to `/free-disney-resort-activities/`
* `/activities?category=campfire` → canonical to `/disney-resort-campfire-schedule/`
* `/activities?category=movie` → canonical to `/disney-world-resort-movie-schedule/`

### Noindex

Low-value combinations should usually be noindexed:

* Resort + free + category + date combinations
* Sort-order variations
* Filter combinations with too few results
* Expired date filters
* Internal preview/gated pages
* Search-result pages with no unique intro, no unique title, and no durable search demand

Google recommends organizing sites logically, using descriptive URLs, and using canonical URLs when duplicated pages exist. ([Google for Developers][1])

## 5.2 Sitemap and robots

I could not verify the robots.txt or sitemap.xml through the public fetcher I used, so I would not call that a confirmed issue. But you should verify directly in Google Search Console and with a command-line fetch.

Recommended sitemap structure:

* `sitemap-pages.xml`
* `sitemap-guides.xml`
* `sitemap-resorts.xml`
* `sitemap-activities-evergreen.xml`
* `sitemap-current-schedules.xml`

For schedule pages, be careful. Do not create thousands of short-lived event URLs that become thin or expired. Use stable evergreen activity pages plus current schedule modules.

## 5.3 Canonicals

Set a canonical URL on every page.

Rules I’d use:

* Homepage canonical → homepage.
* `/activities` canonical → `/activities`.
* High-value topic pages canonical to themselves.
* Parameter/filter pages canonical to the closest clean landing page.
* Expired one-off event pages canonical to the evergreen activity page or relevant resort page.
* Preview/gated pages should be noindexed or removed from public discovery.

I found an activity URL that led to a preview/site-gate page. That may be intentional, but from an SEO perspective, public indexed preview or gated URLs should be cleaned up. ([After the Parks][22])

## 5.4 Structured data

Add JSON-LD structured data. Google says structured data helps it understand page content and can enable rich results, and it recommends JSON-LD where possible. ([Google for Developers][23])

Use:

* `Organization`
* `WebSite`
* `SearchAction`
* `BreadcrumbList`
* `Article` or `BlogPosting` for guides
* `FAQPage` where FAQ content is visible
* `ItemList` for activity lists
* `Event` only for scheduled activities that meet event markup standards and have clear name, start time, location, availability/status, and offers/pricing
* `Place` or `TouristAttraction` cautiously for resort-related pages, where appropriate

Do not fake schema. If an activity is a general recurring resort offering without a precise event start time, avoid marking it up as a specific event.

## 5.5 Titles and meta descriptions

The current page titles need to be relentlessly aligned with search intent. Examples:

| Page              | Recommended title                                                               |
| ----------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Homepage          | **Disney Resort Activities Today & Tonight | After the Parks**                  |
| Resorts hub       | **Disney World Resort Activity Calendars: Current Activities by Resort**        |
| Activities hub    | **Current Disney World Resort Activities: Movies, Campfires, Crafts & More**    |
| Movie schedule    | **Disney Movies Under the Stars Schedule at Walt Disney World Resorts**         |
| Campfire schedule | **Disney Resort Campfire Schedule: Locations, Times & Current Listings**        |
| Free activities   | **Free Disney Resort Activities Today: Movies, Campfires, Crafts & More**       |
| No-park-day guide | **Disney World No-Park-Day Guide: Resort Activities, Free Plans & Itineraries** |
| First-night guide | **First Night at Your Disney Resort: Easy Arrival-Day Activities & Plans**      |

Meta descriptions should be plain and useful:

> See current Disney World resort activities today and tonight, including movies, campfires, crafts, pool games, free activities, weather-friendly options, and source verification dates.

Google may rewrite snippets, but good visible page text and titles still help Google understand the page. Google’s SEO documentation emphasizes clear titles, useful snippets, and helpful content created for users. ([Google for Developers][1])

# 6. Content quality audit

## 6.1 The site has a strong data moat

The homepage shows recent verification and verified activity counts. ([After the Parks][2]) Resort pages show source dates, activity counts, schedule windows, official-source status, and caveats. ([After the Parks][24]) Activity detail pages include timing, resort, cost, reservation notes, accessibility/transportation notes, weather fit, source freshness, and confirmation advice. ([After the Parks][25])

That is the thing to protect. The database is the moat.

## 6.2 The site also needs aggressive editorial QA

The fastest way to lose the SEO advantage is letting templated or malformed content remain visible.

Fix examples:

* All-Star Music says there is no verified tonight listing, while later listing evening activities. ([After the Parks][5])
* A Grand Floridian filtered page includes awkward/truncated snippets like “joy the warm glow,” cut-off phone text, and malformed schedule text. ([After the Parks][26])
* A Family Activities page says “is a activity activity” and “Other activity activities.” ([After the Parks][6])
* A Winter-Themed Activities page exposes internal planning language: “Fixed-time activities should not receive exact timing recommendations.” ([After the Parks][25])

Create an automated content QA script that fails deployment if public pages contain patterns like:

* `activity activity`
* `a activity`
* `Fixed-time activities should`
* `Soft planning only`
* `Coa:`
* broken phone strings
* missing words at the start of sentences
* repeated generic intros
* empty “tonight” modules when evening rows exist
* “no verified listing” contradictions
* duplicate H1s
* pages with fewer than a minimum number of unique words
* pages with title/H1 mismatch
* pages with visible internal labels such as “data pattern,” “severity,” or internal QA notes unless rewritten for users

## 6.3 Make pages feel less generated

The First Night guide has the right SEO target and useful modules, but it also has repeated “Start here” sections with very similar language. ([After the Parks][27])

For priority guides, add more human editorial value:

* “Best for families arriving after 5 PM”
* “Best for adults without kids”
* “Best if your room is not ready”
* “Best if it is raining”
* “Best if you are staying at a Skyliner resort”
* “Best if you do not want to transfer buses”
* “What I would skip”
* “Common mistakes”
* “Sample 2-hour plan”
* “Sample 4-hour plan”
* “Low-energy plan”
* “Stroller-friendly plan”

This is how you compete with high-authority blogs: keep the data advantage, but add real editorial judgment.

The winning strategy is:

> **Own the long-tail, current-intent Disney resort activity market first. Then use that authority to expand into broader no-park-day and no-ticket planning searches.**

Do not start by trying to beat AllEars or Disney Food Blog for “things to do at Disney World.” Start by becoming impossible to beat for:

* “Disney resort activities tonight”
* “Disney World resort activity calendars”
* “Disney resort movie schedule”
* “Disney resort campfire schedule”
* “[resort] activity calendar”
* “[resort] movie schedule”

My overall verdict: **high upside, but the site must clean up programmatic-content artifacts and faceted-indexation risk before scaling aggressively.** Once that is fixed, the site has a credible path to becoming the best organic result for Disney resort activity searches.
